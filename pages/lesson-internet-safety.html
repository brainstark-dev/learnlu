Internet Safety: Passwords, Scams and Viruses

Stay safe online. Learn how to create strong passwords, recognise scam emails, avoid viruses, and protect your personal information.

Why Online Safety Matters

Every year, millions of people lose money, personal data, or important files because of online threats. The good news: most attacks succeed only because people don't know what to look for. A few simple habits make you dramatically safer.

🧠 Analogy

Online safety is like locking your house. You don't need to be a security expert — you just need to lock the doors, close the windows, and not hand your keys to strangers. Most burglars (hackers) move on when they find a locked door and look for easier targets.

Creating Strong Passwords

Weak passwords are the number-one way people get hacked. A strong password has three qualities: it is long (at least 12 characters), complex (mix of letters, numbers, symbols), and unique (not used on any other account).

🌍 Weak vs Strong Passwords
  • ❌ Weak: john1990 — your name and birth year (guessed in seconds)
  • ❌ Weak: password123 — the most common password in the world
  • ✅ Strong: BlueSky#Mango!47 — easy to remember, hard to crack
  • ✅ Stronger: Use a passphrase: "My dog ate 3 mangoes in 2019!" → MdA3mi2019!
✅ Password Manager

You need a different password for every account — but no one can remember 50 passwords. Use a free password manager like Bitwarden (free) or Google Password Manager (built into Chrome). It remembers all your passwords securely. You only need to remember one master password.

Two-Factor Authentication (2FA)

2FA adds a second layer of security. Even if someone steals your password, they still can't get in without a second code sent to your phone. Enable 2FA on your email, bank, and social media accounts — it takes 2 minutes to set up and dramatically improves your security.

Common Online Threats

  • Viruses & Malware: Malicious software that damages your computer, steals data, or locks files for ransom. Spread through downloads, email attachments, and infected USB drives.
  • Phishing: Fake emails or websites pretending to be your bank, Google, or a trusted company — designed to trick you into entering your password or sending money.
  • Social Engineering: Someone calls pretending to be from "Microsoft" or your bank, saying there's a problem and they need your password to fix it. Legitimate companies will never ask for your password.
  • Ransomware: Malware that locks all your files and demands payment to restore them. Back up your files regularly to protect against this.

🧠 Quick Quiz: Someone calls claiming to be from Microsoft saying your computer has a virus. What should you do?

📌 Key Takeaways

  • Use long, unique passwords for every account — use a password manager to remember them
  • Enable two-factor authentication (2FA) on all important accounts
  • Never share your password with anyone — not even people who claim to be from tech support
  • Back up important files to protect against ransomware
  • Keep your operating system and antivirus updated
