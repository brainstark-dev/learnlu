How to Spot a Phishing Scam — Email, SMS and Fake Websites

Learn how to identify fake emails, fraudulent text messages, and scam websites that try to steal your money and personal information.

What is Phishing?

Phishing (pronounced "fishing") is a cyberattack where criminals send fake messages — pretending to be a trusted source like your bank, Google, or a delivery company — to trick you into clicking a link, entering your password, or sending money.

🧠 Analogy

Just like a fisherman throws out bait hoping a fish will bite, phishing attackers send out millions of fake emails hoping a few people will take the bait. They don't need most people to fall for it — just one in a thousand is enough to make it profitable.

7 Warning Signs of a Phishing Email

  1. Urgency and panic: "Your account will be closed in 24 hours!" — Scammers create panic to stop you thinking clearly.
  2. Suspicious sender address: The email says it's from "Apple" but the address is support@apple-security-alert.ru — real Apple emails come from @apple.com only.
  3. Spelling and grammar errors: "Dear Costumer, we have noticed a suspicious activitey on you're account" — legitimate companies proofread their emails.
  4. Requests for personal information: No legitimate bank or company will ever ask for your full password, PIN, or account number by email.
  5. Suspicious links: Hover over a link (don't click!) — if it shows a strange URL that doesn't match the company's real website, it's a phishing link.
  6. Generic greetings: "Dear Customer" or "Dear User" instead of your actual name — your bank knows your name.
  7. Unexpected attachments: An invoice or delivery notice you weren't expecting — clicking the attachment could install malware.
🌍 Real Phishing Example

You receive an email: "Your GTBank account has been suspended due to suspicious activity. Click here to verify your details within 24 hours or your account will be permanently closed." The link goes to gtbank-security-verify.com (not the real gtbank.com). If you click and enter your internet banking username and password, criminals now have full access to your account.

What to Do If You Receive a Suspicious Email

  1. Do NOT click any links
  2. Do NOT download any attachments
  3. Do NOT reply with any personal information
  4. If you're worried it might be real, go directly to the company's website by typing their URL yourself (e.g., type gtbank.com in your browser — don't click the email link)
  5. Mark the email as spam/phishing in your email app
  6. Delete the email

🧠 Quick Quiz: You receive an email from "paypal-security@account-verify.com" asking you to confirm your PayPal password. What do you do?

✅ Golden Rule

If an email, text, or phone call creates a sense of URGENCY and asks you to CLICK something or PROVIDE information — slow down. That combination (urgency + action required) is the classic phishing formula. Take a breath, and verify through official channels before doing anything.

📌 Key Takeaways

  • Phishing = fake messages pretending to be trusted sources to steal your information
  • Red flags: urgency, bad grammar, suspicious sender, requests for passwords
  • Hover over links before clicking to see the real destination URL
  • When in doubt, go directly to the company's website yourself — don't use the email link
  • Legitimate companies never ask for your password via email or phone
