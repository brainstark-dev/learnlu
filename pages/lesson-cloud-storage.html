Cloud Storage Explained: Google Drive, OneDrive & Dropbox

Cloud storage lets you save files on the internet instead of only on your computer — meaning you can access them from any device and never lose them if your laptop breaks. This lesson explains everything you need to know.

What is Cloud Storage?

Cloud storage means saving your files on powerful computers (called servers) owned by companies like Google, Microsoft, or Dropbox — and accessing them over the internet. Despite the mystical name, "the cloud" is simply someone else's computer in a data centre building.

Cloud storage is like a safe deposit box at a bank. Instead of keeping all your valuables at home (your hard drive — which could be stolen, flooded, or burned), you keep important items at the bank (the cloud). You can access your box from any branch (any device), and if your house burns down (your computer crashes), your valuables are perfectly safe at the bank.

Why Use Cloud Storage? 5 Key Benefits

✓
Access anywhere: Your files on your phone, laptop, tablet, or a friend's computer — just log in
✓
Automatic backup: If your laptop breaks, your files are safe in the cloud
✓
Easy sharing: Share a document link instead of emailing a large attachment
✓
Collaboration: Multiple people can edit the same Google Doc simultaneously in real-time
✓
Free storage: Google gives 15GB free, OneDrive gives 5GB free, Dropbox gives 2GB free

Using Google Drive — Step by Step

1

Go to drive.google.com

Sign in with your Gmail account. You immediately have 15GB of free storage.

2

Upload files

Drag any file from your computer into the Drive window. Or click "+ New" → "File upload." The file is now safely stored online.

3

Create Google Docs/Sheets

Click "+ New" → Google Docs to create a word processing document, or Google Sheets for a spreadsheet. These are free alternatives to Microsoft Word and Excel — and they save automatically.

4

Share files

Right-click any file → "Share" → type someone's email address. Choose "Viewer" (read-only), "Commenter" (can comment), or "Editor" (can change). Send the link.

You write your CV in Google Docs. Your friend in another city reviews it and adds suggestions directly in the document — you both see the changes in real time. You then download it as a .docx file to email to employers. No version confusion, no emailing files back and forth — just one shared document that both of you can edit.

Understanding Sync

Sync means the cloud version and your computer's local version are always kept identical automatically. When you edit a file on your laptop, the cloud copy updates in seconds. When you change it on your phone, the laptop copy updates too.

Install the Google Drive desktop app on your Windows computer. A "Google Drive" folder appears in File Explorer. Anything you put in that folder is automatically synced to the cloud. You can work offline — changes sync automatically when you reconnect to the internet.
