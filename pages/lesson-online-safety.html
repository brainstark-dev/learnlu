Online Safety: Protecting Yourself on the Internet

The internet is an amazing tool — but it has real dangers too. This lesson teaches you the habits that keep you safe from hackers, scammers, and data theft. Most online crimes are preventable with simple awareness.

The Main Online Threats

Understanding the dangers is the first step to avoiding them. Here are the four threats that affect the most people:

1

Phishing — Fake Emails and Websites

Criminals send emails pretending to be your bank, Google, or government. They create fake websites that look identical to real ones. The goal: steal your username and password when you "log in" to the fake site.

2

Malware — Harmful Software

Programs that damage your computer, spy on you, or steal your files. Spread through downloads from untrustworthy websites, email attachments, or infected USB drives.

3

Weak Passwords — The Easiest Way In

Hackers use automated tools that can guess "password123" in milliseconds. One compromised account often leads to others if you reuse the same password.

4

Oversharing Personal Information

Posting your phone number, home address, or daily routine on social media gives criminals the information they need to target you.

Creating Strong Passwords

A strong password is your first line of defence. Follow these rules:

✓
Length over complexity: 15+ characters beats a short complicated one. "ILoveFreshMangoes2025!" is stronger than "P@55w0rd"
✓
Never use personal info: No birthdays, names, phone numbers, or pet names
✓
Unique for each site: If one site is hacked, only that account is compromised
✓
Use a password manager: Apps like Bitwarden (free) or 1Password remember all your passwords securely

The Passphrase Method: Pick 4 random words and connect them. "CoffeeLionBridgeRain" is 20 characters long, easy to remember, and would take a computer billions of years to crack. Add a number and symbol: "CoffeeLionBridgeRain7!" — practically unbreakable.

Two-Factor Authentication (2FA) — Your Second Lock

Two-factor authentication (2FA) adds a second layer of security. After entering your password, you must also enter a code sent to your phone (or generated by an app). Even if a hacker steals your password, they can't access your account without your phone.

2FA is like a bank that needs both your card AND your PIN. Even if someone steals your card (password), they can't access the account without also knowing your PIN (the 2FA code). Enable 2FA on Gmail, your bank, and any important account today.

Spotting Phishing Attempts

Before clicking any link in an email, check these warning signs:

Red FlagReal Example
Sender email doesn't match the companyEmail claims to be from "GTBank" but comes from gtebank-alert@hotmail.com
Urgent or threatening language"Your account will be SUSPENDED in 24 hours unless you verify NOW"
Generic greeting"Dear Valued Customer" instead of your actual name
Suspicious linksHover over the link — it shows http://gtbank.verify-now.ru instead of gtbank.com
Requests for personal infoReal banks NEVER ask for passwords or card numbers via email
Poor spelling and grammarErrors suggest the email wasn't written by a professional organisation

Safe Browsing Habits

✓
Always check for 🔒 HTTPS and the padlock before entering passwords or payment info
✓
Never use public WiFi (cafes, airports) to log into bank accounts or email without a VPN
✓
Keep your browser and OS updated — security patches close known vulnerabilities
✓
If an offer seems too good to be true ("You've won an iPhone!"), it is definitely a scam
✓
Think before you click — pause for 3 seconds before clicking any unexpected link

📌 Key Takeaways

✓
Use long, unique passwords for every account — use a password manager to remember them
✓
Enable 2FA on email, banking, and social media accounts
✓
Check the sender's email address before clicking links
✓
Real organisations never ask for passwords via email
✓
If in doubt — don't click. Go directly to the website instead.
