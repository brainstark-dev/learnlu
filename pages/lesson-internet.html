How the Internet Works — Explained Simply

Billions of people use the internet every day — but very few understand how it actually works. This lesson breaks it all down using everyday comparisons you will immediately understand.

What is the Internet?

The internet is a massive, global network of computers and devices that are all connected together. Think of it as the world's largest library, post office, phone network, and marketplace — all combined into one system that is available 24 hours a day, 7 days a week.

"The internet is like the world's road system. Every computer is a building, and internet cables are the roads connecting them. When you visit a website, it's like driving from your house to a shop, picking up information, and driving back home."

📊 SVG Diagram Your device → WiFi → ISP → Internet → Website

The internet is NOT the same as:

  • ❌ WiFi — WiFi is just one method of connecting TO the internet (the on-ramp to the highway)
  • ❌ The Web (websites) — websites are just one type of content that runs on top of the internet
  • ❌ Your phone data — that's another way to connect, but still uses the same global internet infrastructure

When you send a WhatsApp message from Lagos, Nigeria to someone in London, UK: your message travels through fibre optic cables under the ocean — crossing thousands of miles in under a second. This is the internet at work.

How Do Websites Work?

Every website is made of files (text, images, videos) that are stored on a powerful computer called a server. A server is just a computer that runs 24/7 and is designed to send information to anyone who asks for it.

1

You type a web address (URL)

For example, you type www.google.com into your browser.

2

Your computer finds Google's address (DNS Lookup)

The internet's phone book (called DNS) converts "google.com" into a numerical address (called an IP address) like 142.250.64.100. This is how computers actually find each other.

3

Your computer sends a request to Google's server

Your computer sends a message: "Please send me your homepage." This travels through cables and routers across the world.

4

Google's server sends back the webpage files

The server sends back the HTML, CSS, and images that make up the Google homepage.

5

Your browser displays the webpage

Your browser (Chrome, Firefox, etc.) reads those files and draws the beautiful website you see on screen.

Visiting a website is like ordering food at a restaurant: (1) You order from the menu (type the URL). (2) The waiter finds the chef (DNS finds the server). (3) The kitchen prepares your meal (server sends the files). (4) The waiter brings it to your table (browser receives data). (5) You eat (browser displays the page). The whole process takes about 0.1–2 seconds!

WiFi vs Internet vs Mobile Data

These three terms confuse many beginners. Here is the clear distinction:

TermWhat it isAnalogy
🌐 InternetThe global network of all connected computersThe highway system
📶 WiFiWireless technology to connect your device to a local network/routerThe on-ramp to the highway
📱 Mobile DataCellular connection (4G/5G) to the internet via phone towersA helicopter route to the highway
🔌 EthernetA physical cable connecting your device directly to the routerA private dedicated road

Your WiFi can be on but your internet can be down. This happens when your router is working (you can connect devices to it) but your ISP (Internet Service Provider) has an outage. In this case, your phone can connect to WiFi but still can't open any websites — because the road (internet) is blocked, even though your car (device) started fine.

Web Browsers — Your Window to the Web

A web browser is the app you use to visit websites. It's the tool that requests web pages, receives the files, and displays them visually on your screen. Without a browser, you'd see raw code — not beautiful websites.

🔵

Google Chrome

The most popular browser worldwide. Fast, supports many extensions. Made by Google. Best for most users.

🦊

Mozilla Firefox

Open-source and privacy-focused. Great alternative to Chrome. Very customisable.

🔷

Microsoft Edge

Built into Windows. Modern and fast, uses the same engine as Chrome. Good default choice.

🧭

Safari

Apple's browser, built into Mac and iPhone. Fastest on Apple devices and great for battery life.

Every browser has an address bar at the top where you type web addresses (URLs). URLs always start with https:// — the "s" stands for secure. Always check for the padlock icon 🔒 and "https" before entering personal information like passwords or bank details on a website.

Email — Electronic Mail

Email (electronic mail) lets you send messages, files, and images to anyone in the world instantly, for free. Each email address is unique — it's like a personal postal address, but digital.

"Email is like the postal system, but instant. Your email address is your house address. The email server is the post office. Sending an email is like dropping a letter in a postbox — it arrives at the other person's inbox (mailbox) in seconds, not days."

An email address has two parts separated by an @ symbol:

yourname@gmail.com
