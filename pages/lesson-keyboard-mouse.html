Keyboard & Mouse: Your Computer's Most Important Input Devices

The keyboard and mouse are how you tell the computer what to do. Mastering these two tools is the foundation of computer confidence — and it's much simpler than most beginners think.

The Keyboard

The keyboard is the primary text-input device for a computer. The layout used worldwide is called QWERTY — named after the first six letters on the top row. This layout was originally designed for typewriters in the 1870s and has stayed the standard ever since.

The keyboard is like a very advanced remote control for your computer. Just as a TV remote has different buttons for different functions (volume, channels, power), the keyboard has keys for letters, numbers, commands, and shortcuts — each doing something specific when pressed.

The Five Groups of Keys

1

Alphanumeric Keys

The letters (A–Z) and numbers (0–9) in the main area. These are the keys you use for typing text and numbers in any document, email, or form.

2

Function Keys (F1–F12)

The row at the very top. F1 usually opens Help. F2 renames a selected file. F5 refreshes a webpage. F11 goes fullscreen. Their exact function varies by program.

3

Modifier Keys

Shift (types capital letters and symbols like !@#). Ctrl (Control — used for shortcuts like Ctrl+C to copy). Alt (Alternative — used in shortcuts). Windows key ⊞ (opens Start Menu, used in system shortcuts).

4

Navigation Keys

Arrow keys (←↑→↓) move the cursor. Home jumps to the start of a line. End jumps to the end. Page Up/Page Down scrolls by a full screen. Delete deletes the character to the right of the cursor.

5

Numeric Keypad

The number pad on the right side of full-size keyboards. Press Num Lock to activate it. Useful for quickly entering numbers — like a calculator built into your keyboard.

Backspace vs Delete: Backspace (top-right of main area) deletes the character to the LEFT of your cursor — like erasing your last typed letter. Delete deletes the character to the RIGHT. If you mistype a word, press Backspace to erase backwards. If you want to remove the next character, press Delete.

The Mouse

The mouse moves a pointer (cursor) on your screen and lets you click, select, and interact with everything you see. Most mice have three controls: the left button, the right button, and the scroll wheel.

Building Mouse Confidence

Many beginners find precise mouse control tricky at first. Here are three practices that will build your skills quickly:

✓
Hover before clicking — move the cursor over something and pause. Look for a tooltip or colour change before clicking to confirm you're in the right place.
✓
Click once, wait — single-click selects. Double-click opens. Many beginners accidentally triple-click by being too fast.
✓
Right-click everything — right-clicking shows what's possible. It's one of the best ways to discover hidden options in any program.

Trackpads on Laptops

Laptops have a built-in trackpad (touchpad) instead of a separate mouse. Your finger is the cursor controller. Tap to click, two-finger tap to right-click, two-finger swipe up/down to scroll.

If you find the trackpad frustrating, plug in an external mouse via USB — it plugs in instantly with no setup on any laptop. Most beginners find a physical mouse much easier to use than a trackpad.
